# Specforge V2 Requirements Specification

## Status

Accepted

## Purpose

This document defines the Requirements Specification artifact used by Specforge.

The Requirements Specification is responsible for describing what a system must do from a business and product perspective.

Its purpose is to transform project knowledge into clear, actionable and testable requirements.

Requirements Specifications are one of the primary outputs generated by Specforge.

---

# Introduction

Many projects fail because requirements are:

- incomplete
- ambiguous
- contradictory
- disconnected from business goals

Traditional requirement documents often become large collections of feature descriptions with little traceability.

Specforge introduces a knowledge-driven Requirements Specification model.

Requirements should originate from discoveries, decisions and product goals.

---

# Definition

## Requirements Specification

A Requirements Specification defines the expected behavior of a system.

A Requirements Specification answers:

- What problem are we solving?
- What must the system do?
- What outcomes are expected?
- What constraints must be respected?

A Requirements Specification does not define implementation.

Implementation belongs to Design and Architecture Specifications.

## Business Rules

Defines rules governing behavior.

Examples:

- eligibility rules
- calculation rules
- validation rules

---

## Acceptance Criteria

Every significant requirement should have acceptance criteria.

Acceptance criteria should be:

- measurable
- observable
- testable

Format:

Given
When
Then

or equivalent.

---

## Assumptions

Lists assumptions made during generation.

Assumptions should be minimized.

---

## Dependencies

Lists dependencies.

Examples:

- external APIs
- third-party systems
- existing modules

---

## Risks

Known uncertainties.

---

## Open Questions

Unresolved issues.

Candidates for clarification.

---

## Traceability References

Links to:

- Discoveries
- Decisions

- Constraints

# Requirement Quality Standards

Every requirement should be:

## Clear

Single interpretation.

## Atomic

One requirement per statement.

## Testable

Can be validated.

## Traceable

Can be linked to source knowledge.

## Necessary

Supports a business objective.

## Consistent

Does not conflict with other requirements.

# Anti-Patterns

## Feature List

Requirements reduced to a feature inventory.

## Missing Rationale

Requirements without explanation.

## Hidden Business Rules

Business logic buried inside descriptions.

## Untraceable Requirements

Requirements without origins.

## Ambiguous Requirements

Multiple interpretations possible.
